# What International Teachers Actually Need

## Source audit

Sources reviewed:
- International Schools Review patterns: review prompts and public discussion about leadership, honesty, workload, housing, salary, and parent/student culture.
- Job platform patterns from Search Associates, ISS-Schrole, and school vacancy pages: benefits most often foregrounded are salary, housing, flights, medical insurance, tuition remission, visas, and professional development.
- Teacher community discussions on TES forums and Reddit communities such as r/Internationalteachers and r/TeachersInternational.
- Public Facebook-group topics for international school teachers and educators, especially location-specific threads about housing, family logistics, visas, and contract risk.

Cross-source takeaway:
Teachers are not mainly asking for inspiration. They are trying to reduce downside risk before signing. The most repeated needs are: whether the package really converts into savings, what leadership is actually like, whether housing is livable, how demanding the school is day to day, what family life will look like, and how hard it is to leave or renew.

## Contract terms and benefits comparison
**What teachers want to know:** Base salary, net pay, housing setup, flights, insurance, tuition for dependents, shipping, settling-in allowance, contract length, notice period, renewal terms, and what is actually in writing versus verbally promised.
**How often it comes up:** High
**Stool currently:** Partially addresses
**Gap detail (if any):** Salaries and package signals exist, but stool does not yet compare full contract terms, dependent benefits, renewal clauses, shipping, gratuity, or notice-period risk.

## School management style and leadership quality
**What teachers want to know:** Whether leaders are transparent, honest, instructional, reactive, supportive, and likely to protect staff when pressure rises.
**How often it comes up:** High
**Stool currently:** Partially addresses
**Gap detail (if any):** My School and school reviews target this well, but coverage depends on review volume and there is no strong summary layer yet for schools with thin data.

## Housing quality
**What teachers want to know:** Whether housing is provided or an allowance, plus neighborhood, commute, apartment quality, furnishings, family suitability, internet reliability, and whether the allowance covers reality.
**How often it comes up:** High
**Stool currently:** Gap
**Gap detail (if any):** Stool tracks housing as a package input, but not the lived quality of accommodation or the gap between official allowance and real market options.

## Student behavior and classroom culture
**What teachers want to know:** How students participate, how much structure they expect, parent pressure, classroom norms, and what will feel different from their current context.
**How often it comes up:** High
**Stool currently:** Addresses
**Gap detail (if any):** Classroom Guide is strong here. The next gap is breadth: more behaviors, more examples, and clearer links between culture and day-to-day teaching decisions.

## Curriculum familiarity and resources
**What teachers want to know:** Whether the school uses a curriculum they can teach confidently, what planning support exists, how resourced departments are, and whether the curriculum is real or just a label in recruitment copy.
**How often it comes up:** Medium
**Stool currently:** Gap
**Gap detail (if any):** Curriculum is mostly a filter today. Stool does not yet surface curriculum fit, resource quality, onboarding support, or how faithfully the curriculum is implemented.

## Work-life balance and hours expectations
**What teachers want to know:** Teaching load, meetings, duties, weekend expectations, event load, cover frequency, reporting burden, and whether the school respects time.
**How often it comes up:** High
**Stool currently:** Partially addresses
**Gap detail (if any):** Stool captures some workload signal through diagnostics and school reviews, but it does not yet turn that into a clear work-life benchmark teachers can compare across schools and countries.

## Community and social life for expats
**What teachers want to know:** Whether it is easy to build a life outside school, make friends, travel, date, find communities, and avoid isolation.
**How often it comes up:** Medium
**Stool currently:** Partially addresses
**Gap detail (if any):** The Place leg covers quality of life, safety, and expat suitability, but not social texture, loneliness risk, or the difference between a livable city and a genuinely enjoyable one.

## Partner employment opportunities
**What teachers want to know:** Whether a spouse or partner can work legally, what sectors exist, how realistic dependent visas are, and whether the move is viable on one income.
**How often it comes up:** High
**Stool currently:** Gap
**Gap detail (if any):** No partner-employment or dual-career support exists yet. This is a major blind spot for teachers moving with spouses.

## Children's schooling
**What teachers want to know:** Tuition remission, dependent places, waitlists, age-range coverage, SEN support, transport, and whether staff children are actually welcomed.
**How often it comes up:** High
**Stool currently:** Gap
**Gap detail (if any):** Stool does not currently support family-schooling questions beyond broad place and package signals.

## Healthcare quality and insurance
**What teachers want to know:** Insurance scope, hospital quality, routine-care access, maternity coverage, mental-health care, emergency response, and out-of-pocket exposure.
**How often it comes up:** Medium
**Stool currently:** Gap
**Gap detail (if any):** Healthcare is not represented directly. Teachers regularly treat this as a go/no-go issue, especially for families.

## Career development and PD opportunities
**What teachers want to know:** Whether the school builds careers, funds training, supports leadership pathways, and gives teachers room to grow rather than just survive.
**How often it comes up:** Medium
**Stool currently:** Partially addresses
**Gap detail (if any):** Some school-leg and review questions hint at this, but stool does not yet provide a clear PD or advancement signal teachers can use in comparisons.

## Exit strategy and contract-end outcomes
**What teachers want to know:** What happens if the job is bad, how resignations are handled, whether references are weaponized, what completion bonuses exist, and how easy it is to pivot after one contract.
**How often it comes up:** Medium
**Stool currently:** Gap
**Gap detail (if any):** Stool is strong on entry decisions and weak on exit risk. Teachers repeatedly want to know the downside if a move goes wrong.

## Visa stability and political risk
**What teachers want to know:** Document requirements, renewal reliability, dependent visas, labor-law protection, censorship or political shocks, and whether the destination is stable enough for a multi-year plan.
**How often it comes up:** High
**Stool currently:** Gap
**Gap detail (if any):** Safety is present, but visa friction, legal predictability, and political risk are not modeled directly.

## Cost of living vs. take-home pay
**What teachers want to know:** What they can actually save after rent, utilities, schooling, transport, groceries, taxes, and lifestyle costs, not just the headline salary.
**How often it comes up:** High
**Stool currently:** Partially addresses
**Gap detail (if any):** Stool has salary data and cost-of-living data, which is a strong base, but it still lacks a real savings calculator, household scenarios, and allowance realism checks.

## Priority implications for stool

Highest-value gaps to consider next:
1. Housing reality layer: accommodation quality, neighborhood, commute, and allowance sufficiency.
2. Full contract comparison: tuition, insurance, gratuity, contract length, notice period, and dependent benefits.
3. Workload benchmark: hours, duties, meetings, and weekend expectations in a comparable format.
4. Family logistics layer: partner work rights, dependent schooling, healthcare, and visa friction.
5. Exit-risk layer: contract completion, renewal culture, references, and what happens when a posting goes bad.

If stool keeps its current three-leg model, the strongest expansion path is not to replace it. It is to deepen each leg with the practical detail teachers use to decide whether the move is survivable, sustainable, and worth the tradeoff.
