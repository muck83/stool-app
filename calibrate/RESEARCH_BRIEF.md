# Calibrate — Cultural Lens Research Brief

Research compiled April 2026. For use in module content, quiz question design,
simulation scenario writing, and dimension framing. Each section covers the core
cultural dynamic, what teachers typically misread, and the research backing it.

---

## India (`india-ib`)

### Core dynamic: marks as proof of personhood

The Indian education system — particularly CBSE — runs on percentage culture. A score
below 65% is considered below average; above 95% is exceptional. But the cultural
weight attached to these numbers goes far beyond academic measurement. Research
consistently finds that in Indian society, a child's intelligence — and by extension the
family's social standing — is understood to be *read off* the marks sheet. This is not
anti-intellectual; it is a deeply rational response to a labour market and social
hierarchy in which visible, comparable credentials remain one of the few reliable
engines of upward mobility across caste lines.

When an IB teacher presents a portfolio, a narrative feedback sheet, or a rubric that
declines to rank students against each other, the parent who asks "But where does my
child actually stand?" is not rejecting inquiry-based learning. They are asking for the
one piece of information the school's communication has withheld: *comparative position*.

### The elite rebranding layer

A 2020 Tandfonline study ("Branding an 'Inter'national school") examined how a
Bengaluru IB school reconciled "global credentials" with "Indian values." The school
operated within a forward-caste Hindu cultural ethos while pursuing IB Middle Years
accreditation — not despite the contradiction but because of it. "Indian values"
language functions, in this context, as a class-and-caste signal: it tells prospective
families that the school is globally ambitious but socially legible to the dominant
community. A teacher who hears a parent invoke "Indian values" in a PTM is often
hearing a class-and-community-belonging claim, not a curriculum objection.

### What teachers misread

- **"Where does my child stand?"** → Misread as resistance to holistic assessment.
  Actually: a request for comparative data that the system has trained the family to need.

- **Parent silence in PTMs** → Misread as satisfaction or disengagement.
  Actually: deference to teacher authority is performed, not felt. The real conversation
  often happens in WhatsApp parent groups afterward.

- **Excessive follow-up questions after a "warm" meeting** → Misread as anxiety disorder
  or distrust. Actually: the meeting felt inconclusive because no ranking information
  was exchanged.

- **"Indian Values Week" enthusiasm** → Misread as cultural pride. May also be: a
  signal that the school is socially safe for the dominant community group.

### Key research

- Shruti Shankar (2025, SAGE): "International Schools in India and the Emergence of a
  New School-to-University Pipeline" — documents how India's 225+ IB schools (up from
  ~100 in 2014) are increasingly positioned as pipeline institutions for Western
  university admission, not as alternatives to CBSE logic but as extensions of it.
- Tandfonline (2020): "Branding an 'Inter'national school: Fusing 'Indian values' with
  a global diploma" — the core ethnographic study on caste, class, and the IB in
  Bengaluru.
- CBSE grading data: 708 international curriculum schools in 2017-18 → 923 in 2023-24.
  Market projected at $14.67B by 2030 (CAGR ~8.3%).

### Practitioner insight

The strongest frame for teachers: a parent who built their own career on a 94% board
result does not distrust the IB because they are ignorant of inquiry. They distrust it
because no one has yet shown them that it produces comparable outcomes in the
*competitive arena they understand* — university admissions and professional placement.
When you can show them that data, the conversation changes.

## Saudi Arabia / KSA (`ksa-ib`)

### Core dynamic: the selection paradox

A 2024 peer-reviewed study in *Nature — Humanities and Social Sciences Communications*
("How Saudi parents rationalize the choice of school for their children") — based on
semi-structured interviews with Saudi families in private K-12 schools — found that
cultural and religious identity was the **predominant factor** in school selection,
ahead of academic reputation, fees, or facilities. This includes:

- Preference for gender segregation
- Wariness of English displacing Arabic
- Concern that "student voice" and "individual expression" pedagogies undermine
  traditional familial and religious authority structures

Yet the same families are choosing international schools. The apparent contradiction
resolves when you understand the selection logic: they are choosing an institution they
believe can deliver globally recognised credentials *without* requiring the family to
abandon the cultural and religious framework that gives their child's life meaning and
their family its social standing.

This is the paradox a teacher in KSA must hold: the family chose IB *and* they are
worried about what IB will do to their child.

### The language dimension

Research on Saudi children in Riyadh international schools ("Language Shift Among Saudi
Children," ResearchGate 2024) found that children in English-medium settings tend to
shift toward English as their dominant spontaneous language — including at home. Parents
are aware of this risk. When a teacher promotes English-first routines, parent concern
is not about language policy alone; it is about whether the school is a site of slow
cultural displacement.

A complementary study on English-only policy ("Impact of the English-Only Policy on
Learners at International Schools from the Perspective of Teachers and Parents," 2024)
found that English medium had minimal impact on students' religious or cultural *values*
but significantly affected Arabic literacy and language preference. Parents do not
always have access to this distinction — they experience the language shift as more
threatening than the research warrants, but the concern is not irrational.

### Vision 2030 tension

Saudi Arabia's Vision 2030 education reform agenda is creating a novel tension: the
state is actively pushing female workforce participation (66% of STEM university
graduates are now female), English-language competence, and globally competitive
qualifications — while families navigate this modernisation without a clear precedent
for what a "good" education outcome looks like for their daughters and sons in the new
economy. A teacher who sees a parent question "student voice" activities may be seeing
someone navigating this uncertainty in real time, not someone opposed to modern
education in principle.

### What teachers misread

- **Questions about "student voice" or "individual expression"** → Misread as
  conservatism. Actually: legitimate concern about whether the pedagogy undermines
  parental authority in a context where that authority is load-bearing.

- **Preference for formal, structured parent communication** → Misread as emotional
  distance. Actually: appropriate protocol in a high-context culture where informal
  contact implies a social relationship the family may not have chosen.

- **Language policing at home** → Misread as anti-English sentiment. May be:
  a family protecting Arabic literacy while using English as an economic tool.

- **Parent who chose the school enthusiastically and is now asking probing questions**
  → Misread as inconsistency. Actually: the selection paradox in motion — they chose IB
  and they are watching to see whether the cultural bargain holds.

### Key research

- Alfaraidy (2020, SAGE): "Factors Influencing Saudi Parents' Choice of International
  Schools in Saudi Arabia" — foundational quantitative study on selection factors.
- Nature / Humanities and Social Sciences Communications (2024): "How Saudi parents
  rationalize the choice of school for their children" — qualitative semi-structured
  interviews, most current peer-reviewed study.
- Oxford Business Group (2024): Saudi private education market regulatory context.
- Springer (2025): "Education reform and Vision 2030 in Saudi Arabia: challenges and
  pathways" — policy layer.

## Korea (`korea-ib`)

### Core dynamic: zero-sum investment and weaponised accountability

South Korea's education system is built around a single, high-stakes university
entrance exam — the Suneung — that functions as a sorting mechanism for life outcomes.
Two structural facts define the parental psychology that teachers encounter:

1. **Korea's birth rate is 0.78** (2023, the lowest ever recorded for a country).
   Most Korean parents have one or two children. Every parental resource — financial,
   emotional, temporal — is concentrated on a single or double bet. The stakes of
   educational failure are not distributed across multiple children; they are total.

2. **The Child Welfare Act** (2014) defines "emotional abuse" in terms vague enough
   that parents can file formal criminal complaints against teachers for disciplinary
   actions, grade disputes, or even perceived slights. More than 1,200 such accusations
   were filed against teachers in five years; only 1.5% resulted in conviction. The
   practical effect is that many teachers have adopted a posture of radical
   non-intervention to protect themselves.

The 2023 teacher suicide crisis — in which a Seoul elementary teacher took her life
after sustained parental harassment, triggering protests that grew from 5,000 to
300,000 teachers — was not an aberration. It was the visible tip of a documented
systemic problem: 93% of Korean teachers (KTU 2022 survey) said they fear being accused
of child abuse; 63.2% show symptoms of depression; 16% have considered suicide.

### What this means in an international school context

International schools in Korea operate at a deliberate remove from the Suneung
pressure. Classes are in English, counsellors specialise in US/UK admissions. But
Korean parents choosing an international school have not chosen to exit the competitive
framework — they have chosen a different lane within it. The question "Will this get my
child into a good university?" is present in every PTM, even when unasked.

The parental behaviour pattern that Korean teachers have described — calling at night,
filing complaints over minor incidents, demanding that teachers not marry during the
school year — emerges from a hyper-investment logic combined with a legal architecture
that has accidentally made teachers accountable to parents as if parents were clients
with legal standing over a service.

### What teachers misread

- **Intense follow-up after any low grade** → Misread as aggression or hostility.
  Actually: the expression of a parental investment position that has no other release
  valve in the system.

- **Parent who records PTM conversations** → Misread as distrust. May be: legal
  self-protection in a culture where disputes escalate to formal complaints.

- **Student who refuses to attempt something in public without certainty of success**
  → Misread as low confidence. May be: rational risk management in a system where
  visible failure has disproportionate social consequences.

- **Parent who seems satisfied in the meeting but escalates afterward** → Misread as
  bad faith. Actually: high-context communication in which direct confrontation is
  culturally inappropriate; the formal complaint or parent group message is how
  dissatisfaction travels.

### Key research / primary sources

- NPR (Se Eun Gong, October 2023): "South Korea teachers seek protection from
  harassment by students' parents" — comprehensive reported piece with survey data,
  psychiatrist analysis, and teacher testimony. Key quote: *"There's a popular saying
  among teachers these days — if you don't do anything, then nothing will happen to
  you."* (Choi Hyungwook, Parents' Association for Happy Education)

- Berkeley Political Review (January 2024): "Not My Child: Parental Pressure on
  Teachers in South Korea" — structural analysis tracing the crisis to Suneung-based
  zero-sum competition. Cites Dr. Gi-Wook Shin (Stanford) on California's multi-tier
  university model as a counterexample.

- KTU Surveys (2022–2023): 93% fear abuse accusations; 63.2% have depression symptoms;
  16% have considered suicide; 100+ teachers died by suicide 2018–June 2023.

- Korea Herald (2025): "Authorities to directly intervene in teachers' rights violation
  cases" — confirming the issue has not resolved post-2023 legislation.

### Practitioner insight

The proactive communication model cited in the NPR piece (from an alternative school
principal who has avoided harassment by beginning each semester with explicit
parent-teacher expectation-setting conversations) is the most transferable finding for
IB teachers: *name what parents can ask for and what teachers can provide before a
dispute creates the context*. Ambiguity is not neutral in this system — it is
ammunition.

## China (`china-ib`)

### Core dynamic: the Gaokao shadow and the "global dream" transaction

The National Higher Education Entrance Examination (gaokao) is not just an exam; it is
the organising logic of Chinese educational culture. Preparation begins from ninth grade.
University admission through the gaokao determines — or is believed to determine — career
trajectory, marriage prospects, and family honour. The exam is treated as "the most
important indicator of school quality and becomes the deciding factor for the honor or
shame of a family" (USC Center for Health Journalism).

Chinese parents who choose an international school are not abandoning this logic — they
are making a calculated bet that an international pathway (IB → Western university) will
outperform the gaokao pathway *for their specific child* in the *global economy they
expect their child to navigate*. A 2022 study ("The turning tide of the International
Baccalaureate in China," Tandfonline) found 1,103 international schools in China by
2022 (up from 260 in 2010). But the same study documents significant government
pushback: the IB is increasingly framed in state media as an obstacle to the "China
Dream" of national rejuvenation. Parents are choosing international education in a
climate where that choice has begun to carry political valence.

### Three specific friction points

Victoria Quercia (Canadian International School of Hefei, TES Magazine 2025), a
practitioner with six years in Chinese international schools, identifies three recurring
parent concerns that surface in every school system she has observed:

1. **Assessment methods**: Parents prefer frequent standardised testing and letter/
   percentage grades. IB's portfolio-and-presentation model feels unverifiable —
   a parent cannot compare their child to peers or to last year's cohort.

2. **Extracurriculars**: Service learning, outdoor education, creative arts — time spent
   on these activities is time *not* spent on academic preparation. In a system where
   private tutoring routinely extends the school day to 10pm, an extracurricular
   requirement reads as a competitive disadvantage.

3. **STEM weight**: China's competitive labour market rewards STEM credentials heavily.
   Parents perceive the IB's humanities-and-breadth model as underweighting the subjects
   that produce economically legible outcomes.

### The "face" dimension in teacher communication

Cross-cultural communication research consistently identifies *mianzi* (face) as a
structuring force in parent-teacher interaction. Parents will not openly challenge a
teacher's authority in a formal meeting — doing so would cost the parent face by
implying the teacher is incompetent, and cost the teacher face by making the
incompetence public. The practical result is that meetings feel warmer and more resolved
than they are. The real assessment of the school happens in parent networks *after* the
meeting. A teacher who reads a quiet, polite PTM as a sign of satisfaction may be
reading cultural performance.

Confucian values — respect for teacher authority, collective responsibility,
self-cultivation through disciplined effort — remain operative even in families that
have chosen a globally-oriented international school. The tension is not between
"traditional" and "modern" families; it is within the family's own value system, which
holds both Confucian educational virtues and the global ambition simultaneously.

### What teachers misread

- **Parent who asks for more tests** → Misread as resistance to inquiry-based learning.
  Actually: a request for legible, comparative data in the format the family understands.

- **Student who is reluctant to take intellectual risks in class** → Misread as low
  confidence or passivity. May be: rational management of face risk in a context where
  visible error is socially costly.

- **Polite, agreeable PTM → no follow-up concerns** → Misread as resolution.
  May be: face management; the real concerns will surface in the parent network or
  arrive as a formal enquiry later.

- **Parent who requests additional "exam preparation" tutoring alongside the IB**
  → Misread as not trusting the curriculum. Actually: hedging a high-stakes bet in the
  only way the family knows how.

### Key research

- Tandfonline (2022): "The turning tide of the International Baccalaureate in China:
  when global dreams meet national priorities" — the strongest single academic source on
  the IB-China tension, covering both government policy and parent concerns.
- TES Magazine / Victoria Quercia (March 2025): "Parents have doubts about the IB in
  China — but we can win them over" — practitioner account from Hefei, identifying the
  three friction points and a trust-building framework.
- Frontiers in Psychology (2024): "Roles of parents in life satisfaction and educational
  hope among Chinese high school students" — documents how parental expectations shape
  student self-assessment and stress.
- USC Center for Health Journalism: "Coping with Academic Pressure: Navigating Stress
  and Success in Chinese Families."
- PMC (2022): "Longitudinal impact of parent-teacher relationship on middle school
  students' academic achievements in China" — finds parent-teacher relationship quality
  is a significant predictor of academic outcomes, separate from parental
  expectations level.

## Vietnam (`vietnam-ib`)

### Core dynamic: filial piety meets a system in reform

Vietnamese educational culture sits at the intersection of three forces that rarely
resolve cleanly: Confucian-rooted filial piety (education as family honour), a state
system undergoing rapid learner-centred reform, and a fast-growing international school
sector that parents approach with a mixture of aspiration and wariness.

The filial piety dimension is well-documented in peer-reviewed research. A 2020
Frontiers in Psychology study (Polish-Vietnamese comparison) found that *authoritarian
filial piety* — the obligation-driven variant, where a child studies hard out of duty
to parents rather than personal motivation — is a stronger predictor of study engagement
in collectivistic cultures like Vietnam than in individualistic ones. The same research
found that authoritarian filial piety is negatively associated with actual academic
achievement and positively associated with depressive symptoms, because it limits
cognitive independence while increasing pressure. The practical implication: a
Vietnamese student who is visibly hardworking and compliant may be carrying a burden
that has nothing to do with intrinsic motivation and everything to do with not
dishonoring the family.

Academic success in Vietnam is understood as *bringing esteem to the family name*, not
just to the individual. A 2024 three-year longitudinal study of Vietnamese students
(Tran et al., Journal of Rural Medicine, PMC11442093) found that academic stress rose
significantly from Grade 6 to Grade 9 — mean ESSA scores increasing from 46.4 to 53.5
(p<0.001) — with father's educational level as the strongest family-level predictor.
Highly educated fathers place greater expectations tied to familial reputation; children
of these fathers showed significantly higher stress in "despondency" and "workload"
domains. 92.1% of students in the study attended extra (tutoring) classes, and
attending extra classes was independently associated with higher stress. A 2019 Hanoi
study cited in the same paper found a 27% depression rate in secondary students.

### The reform tension

Vietnam's Resolution 29 (2013) mandated a shift from content-transmission to
competency-based, learner-centred education — directly analogous to what IB schools
already practice. But implementation has been uneven. A 2023 Tandfonline study
("Changing teachers' beliefs and practices towards learner-centred education:
experiences from Vietnam's education reforms") documented the gap between policy intent
and classroom reality: many teachers and parents still associate "quality education"
with textbook coverage, structured instruction, and visible academic output. The
expectation gap that IB teachers encounter with Vietnamese parents is therefore not
simply cultural conservatism — it is the collision between a reform agenda the state is
pushing nationally and an assessment philosophy that many families haven't yet seen
deliver the outcomes they trust (university admissions, professional placement).

Vietnam ranks among the top five countries globally for international school growth
(ISC Research); more than 120 international schools operated by 2022, concentrated in
Ho Chi Minh City and Hanoi. The families choosing these schools are predominantly
urban, mobile, and internationally aspiring. They say they want English proficiency,
global pathways, and personal growth. What the research reveals underneath that: they
still carry the marks-as-honour framework that the national system has trained them to
use, and they will reach for it when they feel uncertain about whether the international
school is actually preparing their child competitively.

### What distinguishes Vietnam from China

Both cultures are Confucian-heritage and both have high-stakes university entrance
exams. But three distinctions matter for teachers:

1. **French colonial overlay**: Vietnam's urban elite educational culture — particularly
   in Ho Chi Minh City — carries traces of the French lycée tradition: formal classroom
   structure, strong emphasis on written argument, respect for intellectual credentials.
   This differs from the Chinese model and can make Vietnamese parents more receptive to
   essay-based and portfolio assessment if it is framed as academically rigorous rather
   than alternative.

2. **Socialist education legacy**: Post-1975 Vietnamese education was built on Soviet
   collective models that emphasised conformity, national identity, and teacher
   authority. The shift toward learner-centred models is more recent and more contested
   than in China's urban international schools.

3. **Earlier economic development stage**: Vietnam's "global aspiration" moment for
   international education is roughly where China was a decade ago. The families
   choosing IB schools are often first-generation international school parents —
   they have fewer peers who have been through this system and are navigating with
   less social proof than equivalent Chinese families.

### What teachers misread

- **Student who never asks questions or challenges in class** → Misread as disengaged
  or low-ability. Actually: expressing deference to teacher authority (Confucian norm);
  reluctance to disrupt collective harmony; face-protection for both student and teacher.

- **Parent who praises the school warmly but asks for supplementary test results**
  → Misread as inconsistency. Actually: the textbook/exam framework as a quality marker
  has not been replaced by the school's assessment model — it is running in parallel.

- **Hardworking, compliant student with high anxiety** → Misread as high achiever
  with test nerves. May be: authoritarian filial piety dynamic — working out of family
  obligation, not curiosity, with depression risk the research has documented.

- **Parent who invests heavily in private tutoring alongside international school fees**
  → Misread as not trusting the school. Actually: hedging a high-stakes family
  reputation bet; extra classes are so normalised (92.1% participation rate) that
  stopping them would feel like reducing investment, not reducing pressure.

### Key research

- Tran et al. (2024, Journal of Rural Medicine): "Academic stress among students in
  Vietnam: a three-year longitudinal study" — PMC11442093. The strongest peer-reviewed
  longitudinal data on family factors and stress in Vietnamese secondary students.
- Frontiers in Psychology (2020): "The Implications of Filial Piety in Study Engagement
  and Study Satisfaction: A Polish-Vietnamese Comparison" — documents the authoritarian
  vs. reciprocal filial piety distinction and its effects on academic outcomes.
- PMC (2024): "The interdependence of happiness and filial piety within the family:
  a study in Vietnam" — PMC11016942.
- Tandfonline (2023): "Changing teachers' beliefs and practices towards learner-centred
  education: experiences and lessons from Vietnam's education system reforms."
- WENR (World Education News & Reviews, 2017): "Education in Vietnam" — still the most
  comprehensive English-language overview of the system structure.

## What works: implementation research

The cultural sections above document the *problem*. This section covers what the
evidence says actually shifts parent understanding — relevant for both the teacher
modules (how to have these conversations) and the parent module design.

### Assessment literacy as the lever

The research most directly relevant to parent module design comes from assessment
literacy studies in international school contexts. The core finding: parents who
understand *how* IB assessment works — not just that it exists — are better able to
interpret feedback, have productive conversations with teachers, and support their
children's revision. Technical language is the single most frequently cited barrier to
parental engagement across school types. When schools translate IB rubrics into language
that connects to what parents already care about (university outcomes, skill legibility,
competitive positioning), engagement changes.

John Hattie's synthesis of 800+ meta-analyses identifies parent engagement as having an
effect size of 0.50 on learner achievement — above the 0.40 threshold for meaningful
educational impact. The implication for Calibrate: building parent understanding of the
assessment model is not just relationship management. It is an academically significant
intervention.

### Student-led conferences

Multiple IB school implementation reports identify student-led conferences (SLCs) as
one of the most effective tools for shifting parent understanding of portfolio-based
assessment. The mechanism: when students explain their own learning process to parents,
parents encounter IB assessment as lived experience rather than abstract philosophy.
A 2013 IB PYP blog synthesis notes that SLCs shift parent focus "from summative
assessment grade to the learning process itself." A study by Cheryl Lambert (Austin
Peay State University) found SLCs encourage student ownership and responsibility for
quality outcomes — which parents can observe directly.

The limitation: SLCs alone do not address the *competitive positioning* question. A
parent who sees their child present a beautiful portfolio still wants to know how that
portfolio ranks against other students'. SLCs are most effective when paired with
explicit conversation about university outcomes data.

### The proactive expectation-setting model (Korea)

The most transferable implementation finding from the Korean research is the alternative
school model described in the NPR piece: a psychiatrist-principal who begins each
semester with an explicit parent-teacher session naming what parents can ask for and
what teachers are able to provide. His school has not experienced the harassment pattern
that plagues mainstream Korean schools. The mechanism is simple: ambiguity gets filled
by assumptions, and in high-stakes competitive environments those assumptions are usually
worst-case. Naming the contract explicitly preempts the gap.

This is directly applicable across all four cultures, not just Korea — though it
requires calibration to local face-saving norms (a group session works better in
high-context cultures than an individual letter).

### Parent-to-parent trust

One finding from the cross-cultural engagement literature that cuts across all modules:
parent-to-parent communication is more trusted than school-to-parent communication.
This has two implications for module design. First, the teachers who complete these
modules will be the primary carriers of the school's message to parents — but the most
credible validators of that message are *other parents* who have been through the
system. Second, WhatsApp groups and parent networks are not just noise-channels to
manage. They are the primary epistemic community for most parents in these cultures, and
they run in parallel to whatever the school communicates formally.

### What does not work

- **Philosophy-first explanations of IB assessment** delivered without competitive
  outcome data. Parents in all four cultures will politely receive these and then
  continue to privately rank the school by the only metric they know how to use.

- **One-way information evenings** without dialogue. The research on assessment
  literacy consistently finds that disclosure alone does not produce understanding.
  Parents need to encounter the assessment model actively — through their child's work,
  through conversation, through seeing outcomes — not through a PowerPoint.

- **Assuming a smooth meeting means an resolved parent.** The face-saving dynamic
  operates in India (through WhatsApp groups), Korea (through formal complaints), KSA
  (through formal channels), China (through parent networks), and Vietnam (through
  indirect communication and extra tutoring enrollment). A positive meeting is a
  necessary but insufficient signal.

### Key sources

- Hattie, J. (synthesised): Effect size 0.50 for parent engagement on student
  achievement — above the 0.40 meaningful impact threshold.
- IB PYP Blog (2013, 2015): Student-led conference implementation reports.
- Lambert, C. (Austin Peay State University): Student-led conference and student
  ownership research.
- TES / Quercia (2025): Parent workshop, student-led conference, and university outcomes
  data as trust-building model in Chinese international school context.
- Kim Hyun Soo (psychiatrist, alternative school principal, cited in NPR 2023):
  Proactive expectation-setting model — the most concrete implementation example found.

## Cross-cutting themes

### The "client" shift in international education

A 2023 Tandfonline study of parent-teacher relationships in international schools
(Cyprus context, generalisable) found a consistent pattern: *both* teachers and parents
want meaningful engagement, but the reality is characterised by limited actual
involvement — and an increasing tendency for parents to position themselves as clients
with rights over service delivery rather than partners in education. This client dynamic
is not culturally specific; it is amplified by the fee structure of international
schools. But it interacts differently with each culture:

- **India**: The client logic is tempered by traditional teacher reverence; parents
  exercise the client role indirectly (through WhatsApp groups, third-party complaints)
  rather than directly in meetings.
- **Korea**: The client logic has been legally operationalised through the Child Welfare
  Act — parents have formal complaint mechanisms that put teachers in a structurally
  subordinate accountability position.
- **KSA**: The client logic is present but mediated by a high-context communication
  culture in which direct confrontation would be face-losing for both parties.
- **China**: The client logic coexists with Confucian teacher deference — parents are
  simultaneously investing in the school as a service and treating the teacher as an
  authority figure, which can produce confused signals.

### The inquiry-assessment gap

Across all four cultures, the single most consistent friction point between IB/
international pedagogy and parent expectation is the *legibility of assessment*. IB
assessments are designed to be developmental, holistic, and reflective. Parent
assessment frameworks — shaped by CBSE, gaokao, Suneung, and tawjihiyya — are designed
to be comparative, ranked, and definitive.

This is not a deficit on either side. It is a genuine epistemological gap about what
evidence of learning looks like and who it is for.

### What this means for teacher communication

The research across all four modules converges on a single practical implication: the
teacher who explains assessment philosophy to a parent is answering a different question
from the one being asked. The parent is asking "Is my child competitive?" The teacher
is answering "This is how we measure growth." Both are legitimate — but they are not in
dialogue until the teacher also answers the competitive question, even if the answer is
"Here is what IB graduates achieve in university admissions, and here is where your
child is on that trajectory."
